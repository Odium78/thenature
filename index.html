Effects and Impacts of SDG 17 on the Tech Industry

1. Driving Technology Transfer and Innovation

SDG 17 emphasizes global cooperation in science, technology, and innovation (United Nations, 2015). This encourages technology companies in developed economies to share innovations with developing nations. For instance, the UN Technology Bank helps least developed countries access scientific knowledge and digital tools (United Nations, 2024b). This transfer accelerates innovation in fields like renewable energy, health technology, and artificial intelligence.

2. Expanding Digital Infrastructure and Connectivity

One of the key targets (17.8) focuses on enhancing ICT access. Global internet penetration reached 68% in 2024, but 2.6 billion people remain offline (United Nations, 2024a). This presents both a challenge and an opportunity for the tech industry. Companies like telecom providers, cloud platforms, and device manufacturers benefit from demand for affordable and widespread connectivity solutions.

3. Strengthening Global Tech Partnerships

Public-private partnerships are central to SDG 17 (Target 17.17). Initiatives such as the COVAX digital platforms for vaccine distribution relied on technological cooperation to reach developing nations (IISD, 2024). Likewise, corporate projects like Google’s Project Loon demonstrate how global partnerships can deliver internet access in underserved regions.

4. Encouraging Investment in Tech Development

Mobilizing financial resources (Target 17.3) includes boosting investment in innovation. In 2023, private financial resources to developing countries totaled USD 58.7 billion, a significant rise from 2019 (United Nations, 2024a). This flow of capital has enabled the growth of startups in fintech, edtech, healthtech, and agritech, making the tech industry a direct beneficiary of SDG 17.

5. Building Skills and Capacity

Capacity-building (Target 17.9) stresses the importance of knowledge and skills transfer. Tech firms like Microsoft launched global digital training programs, providing millions with coding, cybersecurity, and cloud skills during and after COVID-19 (Reuters, 2025). These initiatives both expand the skilled workforce and strengthen the industry’s long-term growth.

6. Promoting Ethical and Inclusive Tech Growth

SDG 17’s focus on inclusive partnerships urges tech giants to design affordable and accessible products (United Nations, 2015). This leads to innovations such as local-language platforms, low-cost devices, and assistive technologies. While this expands business opportunities, it also ensures developing nations are not left behind in the digital age.

7. Challenges and Risks

Despite progress, the global tech industry faces challenges under SDG 17. Rising digital dependency on foreign corporations risks weakening local tech sovereignty. Data privacy concerns in cross-border partnerships remain unresolved. Moreover, the digital divide persists, with billions excluded from the benefits of modern technology (United Nations, 2024a).
